When people compare carpet cleaning methods they often want a single winner. The honest answer is that the two main professional approaches, hot-water extraction and low-moisture cleaning, each do some things better than the other. The skill is matching the method to the carpet in front of you.

## How hot-water extraction works

Hot-water extraction, often loosely called steam cleaning, injects a heated cleaning solution deep into the pile and immediately draws it back out under strong suction, flushing the loosened soil with it. It is a thorough, well-proven deep clean and a long-standing professional standard, particularly for heavily soiled synthetic carpet.

The trade-off is water. The carpet is genuinely wetted, so drying can take several hours, longer in cold or humid weather. And if the method is mishandled, over-wetting can cause shrinkage in natural fibres, browning, or damp problems in the backing and the floor beneath. In careful hands it is an excellent method; the risks come from too much water and too little drying.

## How low-moisture cleaning works

Low-moisture systems use far less water. The soil is loosened by a cleaning agent and gentle mechanical action, then removed, with only a fraction of the moisture of a full extraction. Carpets dry much faster and there is far less risk of over-wetting, which is why low-moisture methods are widely regarded as the safer choice for wool and delicate natural fibres.

Texatherm, the system we use, states a typical drying time of 30 to 60 minutes and up to 80 per cent less water than standard extraction. The main trade-off runs the other way: on very heavily soiled synthetic carpet, some operators still prefer the deep flush that extraction gives.

## Residue and re-soiling: the part people miss

Whichever method is used, the thing that most affects how long a carpet stays clean is **residue**. Cleaning product left behind in the pile is sticky, and it attracts soil, so a carpet that was not rinsed or extracted thoroughly can actually re-soil faster than it did before. A low-residue clean, by any method, is what keeps a carpet looking cleaner for longer. This is one reason a careful low-moisture clean can outperform a rushed wet one in the weeks after the job.

## Matching the method to the carpet

- **Wool, Axminster, Wilton, and natural fibres** such as sisal, seagrass, coir, and jute: low-moisture is generally the safer choice, because over-wetting is the main enemy of these materials.
- **Heavily soiled synthetic carpet** (nylon or polyester) in busy areas: the deep flush of extraction can be the right tool, provided the carpet is dried properly afterwards.
- **Homes that need the room back quickly,** or where damp is a concern: low-moisture's fast drying is a real, practical advantage.

Neither method is "best" in the abstract. The right choice depends on the fibre, the level of soiling, and the setting.
