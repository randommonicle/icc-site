Sofas and armchairs quietly collect body oils, dust, food and spills, and unlike a carpet you cannot lift them, soak them and dry them out. That makes the method matter even more: the right approach refreshes upholstery and dries quickly, while the wrong one can watermark or shrink a fabric for good.

## Read the cleaning code first

Most upholstered furniture carries a cleaning code on a label, usually under a seat cushion. It is the single most useful thing to check before you touch a mark:

- **W** — clean with **water-based** products only.
- **S** — clean with **solvent** (dry-cleaning) products only; no water.
- **SW** or **WS** — **either** water- or solvent-based products are safe.
- **X** — **no** water or solvent; vacuum or light brushing only, and a professional clean for anything more.

If there is no code, treat the piece as delicate until a professional has tested it.

## Why the fabric dictates the method

- **Natural fabrics** (cotton, linen, wool) can shrink, watermark or brown if over-wet, much like a natural-fibre carpet.
- **Synthetics** (polyester, microfibre, many modern weaves) are generally more tolerant and clean up well.
- **Delicate fabrics** (viscose, rayon, some velvets) watermark very easily and are often solvent-only — exactly where the cleaning code saves an expensive mistake.

## Between professional cleans

- **Vacuum** upholstery regularly, including under and between cushions.
- **Rotate and plump** loose cushions so they wear evenly.
- **Blot spills straight away**, following the cleaning code, working inwards, and never rub.
- **Avoid over-wetting** and harsh supermarket sprays, which can leave rings or sticky residue.
