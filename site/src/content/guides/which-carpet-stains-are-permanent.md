One of the most useful things a carpet cleaner can do is be honest about what is achievable. Some marks lift completely and the carpet looks new again. Others have changed the fibre itself, and no amount of cleaning will bring the colour back. Knowing the difference saves disappointment, and it is the foundation of treating a stain properly.

## Stain or damage? The crucial difference

A **stain** sits on or within the fibre. Cleaning works by lifting and removing it, and a good professional can shift a great many stains that look hopeless. **Damage** is different: the fibre's own colour has been altered or destroyed. There is nothing to remove, so cleaning cannot help. Bleaching is the classic example. This single distinction explains why some marks come out and others never will.

## Marks that usually come out

Most fresh, water-based spills respond well, especially when treated quickly:

- Tea, coffee, and soft drinks
- Most foods and sauces
- Mud and general soiling, often once it has dried and can be vacuumed
- Fresh pet accidents
- Many washable inks

Even red wine, caught fresh and treated promptly, often lifts far better than people expect. The single biggest factor is time. The sooner a spill is dealt with, the more likely it is to disappear completely.

## Marks that are usually permanent

Some marks are realistically beyond cleaning:

- **Chlorine bleach.** It strips the dye out of the fibre, leaving a lighter or orange-yellow patch. This is permanent, and the only real fixes are re-dyeing or patching.
- **Un-rinsed oxygen, or "oxy", spotters.** Left in the pile, they keep working and bleach the colour out just as permanently as chlorine.
- **Old, set-in pet urine.** Fresh urine often cleans well, but once it has dried and aged, the acids and dyes can permanently alter the carpet colour, and the odour can become extremely difficult to remove.
- **Dried emulsion or latex paint.** Once cured, it bonds to the fibre and usually cannot be removed without damaging the pile.
- **Heat-set stains.** Applying heat, including hot-water cleaning, to a protein or dye stain such as blood, coffee, or fruit juice can set it permanently. These should be treated cool first.
- **Some strong dyes and chemicals.** Mustard and turmeric, certain medicines, some plant dyes, and rust can resist all safe treatment.

## The one exception: olefin

There is one fibre that breaks the bleach rule. Solution-dyed olefin (polypropylene) has its colour locked into the fibre as it is made, so it can resist even bleach without losing colour. It is the most stain-resistant of the common carpet fibres, although it has its own weaknesses: it holds oily soil readily and can flatten in busy areas.

## Why speed matters, and what to do

While a spill is still wet it is just a spill. Once it dries and bonds to the fibre it becomes a stain. To give any spill the best chance:

- **Blot, do not rub.** Rubbing pushes the spill deeper and damages the pile. Press a clean white cloth down to lift the liquid.
- **Work from the outside in** so you do not spread the mark.
- **Use cool water,** never hot, especially on protein stains like blood that heat can set.
- **Avoid harsh or coloured products** that can bleach or add their own dye, and never reach for household bleach.

Then get advice before the mark has a chance to set.
