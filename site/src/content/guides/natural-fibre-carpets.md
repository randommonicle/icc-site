Sisal, seagrass, coir and jute bring texture and a natural look that synthetic carpet cannot match. They are also plant fibres — cellulosic, absorbent, and the most water-sensitive floor coverings in most homes. Cleaned correctly they last for years; cleaned with too much water they shrink, ripple and stain, often permanently.

## The natural fibres and how they behave

- **Sisal** comes from the agave plant. It takes dye well so comes in many colours, but it is the most absorbent of the group and the most prone to watermarking.
- **Seagrass** has a smoother, slightly waxy surface that resists everyday dirt a little better, but it is hard to dye and still cellulosic underneath.
- **Coir**, from coconut husk, is coarse and very tough — a classic doormat and stair fibre.
- **Jute** is the softest and least hard-wearing, and the most prone to browning. It is often used as a carpet backing, which is why over-wet wool carpets brown from the back.

## Why water is the enemy

The common failure with natural fibres is moisture:

- **Browning and watermarks.** Over-wet the fibre and brown compounds wick to the surface as it dries, leaving tide-marks that are difficult or impossible to reverse.
- **Shrinkage and rippling.** Plant fibres swell when wet and can shrink or distort as they dry, leaving a once-flat floor rippled or pulled at the edges.
- **Backing problems.** Many natural-fibre floors have a latex backing that can delaminate or smell if it gets wet.
- **Slow drying.** Anything left damp risks mould and musty odours in the fibre and the floor beneath.

## How we clean natural fibres

The safe approach is the opposite of soaking. We identify the fibre first, then clean with a low-moisture or dry method that uses minimal water, lifts soil with gentle mechanical action, and dries quickly so moisture never has time to cause browning or shrinkage. It is the same principle behind cleaning [wool carpets](/guides/cleaning-wool-carpets) safely, taken a step further because plant fibres are even less forgiving.

## Caring for natural fibres at home

- **Vacuum regularly** with strong suction to lift dry grit before it abrades the weave; go gently with any brush bar on a looped weave.
- **Blot spills immediately** with a dry or barely-damp white cloth, working inwards. Use as little moisture as possible and never pour water on.
- **Never saturate** a natural-fibre floor, and avoid supermarket carpet shampoos, which are too wet and often too alkaline.
- **Manage humidity** in the room — these fibres respond to damp air as well as spills.
