Wool is one of the finest materials a carpet can be made from. It is naturally resilient, warm, flame-resistant, and it hides soil well. It is also a natural protein fibre, and that is exactly why it needs more care than a synthetic carpet when the time comes to clean it. Get the method right and a wool carpet lasts for decades. Get it wrong and the damage can be permanent.

## What makes wool different

Wool is a protein fibre with a scaled outer surface and a natural crimp that gives it spring and bounce underfoot. It is also hygroscopic, meaning it readily absorbs and releases moisture from the air. These qualities make wool comfortable, durable, and good at resisting everyday dirt. They also make it sensitive to heat, moisture, and the wrong chemistry in a way that man-made fibres are not.

## Why wool is easily damaged by the wrong clean

Most wool carpet damage comes down to a few avoidable mistakes:

- **Shrinkage and felting.** Wool's surface scales can interlock when exposed to heat, moisture, and agitation together, especially in alkaline conditions. The result is felting and shrinkage, and it cannot be reversed.
- **Browning.** Many wool carpets have a natural jute backing. Over-wet the carpet and dry it too slowly, and brown compounds wick up from the backing to the surface as it dries. This is known as cellulosic browning, and it is one of the most common results of wet-cleaning natural fibre badly.
- **Colour bleed.** Some wool dyes will run if the carpet is over-wet or cleaned with hot, alkaline solutions.
- **Chemical damage.** Wool dislikes high-alkaline products and is harmed by bleach of any kind. The safe zone for wool is mild and close to neutral.
- **Slow drying.** A carpet left damp for too long risks browning, musty odours, and mould in the backing or the floor beneath.

The common thread is water and harshness. Too much moisture, too much heat, too high a pH, and too much scrubbing are what ruin wool.

## The cleaning approach that suits wool

The safe approach for wool is the opposite of brute force. It uses as little water as the job allows, mild and fibre-appropriate cleaning agents rather than aggressive alkaline products, gentle mechanical action instead of harsh scrubbing, and quick drying so moisture never has time to cause browning or shrinkage.

This is why we favour a low-moisture method for wool and other natural fibres. Low-moisture cleaning is widely recognised as the gentler, safer way to clean materials that over-wetting can damage, and the system we use is rated by its manufacturer as safe for use on wool. The carpet comes out genuinely clean without ever being soaked.

## Looking after wool carpet between cleans

A little routine care keeps a wool carpet looking its best and reduces how often it needs a deep clean:

- **Vacuum regularly** to lift the dry grit that wears fibres down. Use a vacuum with adjustable settings and go gently on looped or Berber wool, where an aggressive brush bar can fuzz the pile.
- **Deal with spills straight away.** Blot, never rub, with a clean white cloth, working from the outside of the spill inwards. Use cool or lukewarm water, never hot.
- **Avoid harsh supermarket spotters** and never use anything containing bleach. If in doubt, blot up what you can and seek advice.
- **Manage sunlight and traffic.** Rotate rugs where you can, and use doormats to catch grit before it reaches the wool.

## When to call a professional

For anything beyond a small fresh spill, a professional clean is the safer route, and it is essential for valuable or antique wool rugs. A good cleaner will identify the fibre and check the backing and dyes before choosing a method, then clean with controlled moisture and proper drying.
